# Combined Section Script  
## Lab Analysis + Ecology (2 presenters, ~4 minutes total)

**Goal:** Deliver taxonomy, biology, ecology, environmental role, and “why it’s weird” in a clear, engaging way after the class has mostly solved the case.

---

# Speaker Roles

- **Speaker A = Lab Analyst**
- **Speaker B = Ecologist**

---

# Speaker A (Lab Analyst) — ~2 minutes

> “Alright investigators, we’ve analyzed the biological evidence collected from the scene.”

> “Microscopic examination shows that this organism produces spores inside specialized sac-like structures called **asci**. This is a defining characteristic of fungi in the phylum **Ascomycota**.”

> “That immediately narrows our suspect list to fungi that reproduce using these structures.”

> “We also observed that the fungus develops a **single elongated fruiting body** emerging from the host organism. This structure is responsible for releasing spores into the environment.”

> “Based on these features — spore production in asci and this specific fruiting structure — we can identify the organism as:  
**Ophiocordyceps unilateralis**.”

> “Taxonomically, this organism is classified as:  
Kingdom: Fungi  
Phylum: Ascomycota  
Genus: Ophiocordyceps  
Species: unilateralis”

> “This species was first described in 1865 and belongs to a group of fungi known for infecting insects.”

> “Now that we know *what* the organism is, the next question is: what exactly is it doing in this ecosystem?”

---

# Speaker B (Ecologist) — ~2 minutes

> “What makes this fungus especially interesting is its relationship with its host.”

> “This species infects **carpenter ants**, specifically those in the genus *Camponotus*, and acts as a **parasitic entomopathogen**, meaning it infects and ultimately kills insects.”

> “It is commonly found in **tropical rainforest environments**, including regions in Southeast Asia and South America, where humidity supports fungal growth and spore dispersal.”

> “Inside the ant, the fungus grows as a network of hyphae, gradually taking over the body. But what makes this species unusual is that it doesn’t just infect the ant — it changes its behavior.”

> “The infected ant is compelled to leave its colony, climb vegetation, and bite down onto the underside of a leaf in what’s called a **‘death grip.’**”

> “This behavior is not random. It positions the ant in an ideal location for the fungus to grow and release spores onto other ants below.”

> “After the ant dies, the fungus produces a fruiting body that emerges from the head and releases spores, continuing the cycle.”

> “Ecologically, this fungus plays an important role by helping regulate insect populations and maintaining balance within the ecosystem.”

> “But what truly makes it one of the weirdest fungi is this combination of infection and behavioral manipulation — essentially turning the host into a vehicle for its own reproduction.”

---

# Transition Line (Optional)

> “So based on the lab evidence and ecological behavior… we can confidently conclude the cause of this case.”
